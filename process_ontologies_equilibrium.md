# Process Ontologies, Equilibrium analysis, and Ego Death

In her essay *The Carrier-Bag Theory of Fiction*, author Ursula K. Le Guin argues that narratives of heroes and heroism have become a “killer story” that threatens the very livability of our age: “Lest there be no more telling of stories at all, some of us out here...think we’d better start telling another one, which maybe people can go on with when the old one's finished...The trouble is, we've all let ourselves become part of the killer story, and so we may get finished along with it.” [[20200407085813]] carrier_bag_theory_of_fiction_leguin She suggests an approach to storytelling focused on containing events. The organizing idea is not linear causality, the straight shot of the hero, but the winding path of proximity.  Instead of a single hero creating a single future, the carrier-bag model allows for complex arcs of temporality, entanglements, and losses of many kinds. [[20200326221713]] earth_stalked_by_man If there are moments that appeart to fit into a hero’s journey, Le Guin urges us to consider it as just another “one of those damned things you have to do in order to be able to go on gathering wild oats and telling stories”. 

Many state-of-the-art computational models are agent-based, which means they explicity model each individual in a population responding to information and behaving in its environment. Hertz et al. introduce these types of models as substance ontologies. Substance ontologies see the world as “objects described by nouns” [[20200403133729]] from_nouns_to_verbs_process_ontologies. These entities interact, but are primary and existed prior to those interactions. 

While these models can be helpful, for describing larger and more complex socio-ecological systems (SES), models based on process ontologies can be more descriptive. Process ontologies see the world as a collection of interdependent processes constituted in relations with other processes. Instead of individual agents, everything is linked in a sequence of events - some causal, others not. Entities are trajectories in possibility spaces, with other events reconfiguring the adjacent possible. 

The major difficulty with switching to a process ontology is the psychological - how do we let go of seeing ourselves as agents? How do we accept the alternatives to heroism, to human-centric, individualistic narratives?
