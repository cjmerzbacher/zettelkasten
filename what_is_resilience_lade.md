# What is Social-Ecological Resilience?

Resilience studied in a lot of fields (1800s)
Engineering: The amount of disturbance a material can sustain before breaking. 

Psychology: Ability to bouce back from negative emotional experience (individually focused), also Development/poverty alleviation

Ecology: Persistence of systems and their ability to absorb change and disturbance (Holling) - system focused version complementary to individually focused version.

Social-ecological systems: Just studying ecosystems by themselves can’t understand sustainability. Must include human systems, actions/interventions, environment, and ecosystem services.

Resilience thinking: A losely organized cluster of concepts and tools for understanding and managing change in complex systems.

Today: social-ecological resilience as capacity to
- Persist
- Adapt 
- Transform

Ball and cup diagram: System rolling around in landscape. Rolling in one regime, with critical threshold after which system transitions into another state: regime shift. (Tipping point)

Regime shift/tipping point: shift that is sudden, large, and difficult to reverse.

Regimeshifts.org

Feedbacks: Causal link: change in something cuases change in something else, chain of causations

Positive and negative feedback loops

- Link sign
- Feedback type (negative) even number of - signs is positive, odd number of negative then negative loop. Stabilizing, balancing.

Regimes stabilized by negative feedback loops. Tipping points are where positive feedback loops take over to change regime.

Is being able to persist always good?
Adaptation: process of change that enables a system to maintain its identity so it can cope with shocks. Often more incremental.

More substantial change: Transformation: shift in a current system to a substantively new and different one.

- Often intentional
- Led by endogenous actors
- Involve priorities different to status quo
- Lead to change across levels of society.
