# Capturring emergent phenomena in social-ecological systems
[https://www.ecologyandsociety.org/vol24/iss3/art11/](https://www.ecologyandsociety.org/vol24/iss3/art11/)

Analyses of SES phenomena require approaches to account for: (1) intertwinedness of systems (2) the ways they give rise to emergent patterns

Linked action situations (AS) tool to capture interactions that are hypothesized to have jointly generated a phenomenon.

Most analyses take either an ecocentric or anthropocentric perspective. 

Focus on system level: describe through aggregate state variables (dynamical systems theory)
* development of system over time
* equilibrium configurations/stable states
* stability of these configurations
* thresholds between configurations

Assume: microscale can be aggregated to macroscale
Leads to unexpected outcomes resulting from heterogeneity of individual or collective actors and ecosystem components and their interactions

SES framework (Ostrom 2007, 2009, 2014): based in collective action theory. Focuses on interactions between resource users, particularly the factors that enable self-organization for sustainable resource use
Core: action situation: situation of groups in which individuals participate in an interaction.

“Human actors in a SE-AS can have diverse needs, interests, values, knowledge, beliefs, skills, access, and opportunity sets, etc. that influence their actions and interactions with the ecological entities (Boons 2013). Ecological entities can have attributes such as spatial distribution, growth rates, quality, etc. that influence their effect on human actors and actions. The relations and interactions between human actors and ecological entities can be material, for instance human actors extract resources or pollute them; ecological entities feed or flood humans. They can also be nonmaterial, e.g., human actors perceive, manage, or are attached to ecological entities, and ecological entities soothe, protect, or are attached to humans (Table 1).”

Interactions lead to outcomes with short/long term consequences

Interactions between people and environment (SES_AS): cultivating/harvesting, converting, recreating, cultural activities, ecological monitoring, polluting

S-AS: Rule making, information sharing, deliberating, conflicts, investing, lobbying, networking, social monitoring, evaluating, competing, trading

E-AS: predation, competition, facilitation, infection, species-habitat interaction, vegetation-soil interaction
