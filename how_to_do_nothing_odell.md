### Surviving Usefulness
“Nothing is harder to do than nothing...For some, there may be a kind of engineer’s satisfaction in the streamlining and networking of our entire lived experience. And yet a certain nervous feeling of being overstimulated and unable to sustain a train of thought lingers.” (ix)

“Such ‘nothings’ cannot be tolerated because they cannot be used or appropriated, and provide no deliverables” (x)

Book is written for “any person who perceives life to be more than an instrument and therefore something that cannot be optimized.” (x
“Solitude, observation, and simple conviviality should be recognized not only as ends in and of themselves, but inalienable rights belonging to anyone lucky enough to be alive.” (xi)

Three movements towards nothing: First, a dropping out. Second, a lateral movement outward towards surrounding things/people. Third, movement downward into place.

“Blasted landscape of neoliberal determinism.” The point “isn’t to return to work refreshed and ready to be more productive, but rather to question what we currently perceive as productive.” (xii)

Odell is “someone who gets invited to tech conferences but who would rather be out bird-watching.” Still in form of self branding, just framed in opposition to tech maximalism.

The parable of the Useless tree, passed by by carpenters and woodcutters. “If I had been of some use, would I ever have grown this large” (xv) Distinction between usefulness and worth.

To **resist in place**: make oneself a shape that cannot be so easily appropriated. Maintenance as productivity, importance of nonverbal communications, and mere experience of life. Self that changes over time and has fuzzy boundaries.

“The present grows out of the past.” Rootedness. Technology as a way to uproot like a pot. Keeps us from needing roots. Wendell Berry?

“Chain-store aesthetic of the visual”

How can we be citizens of a bioregion?

Capitalism and climate change as problems that “coproduce” each other.

“Why is it that the modern idea of productivity is so often a frame for what is actually the destruction of the natural productivity of an ecosystem?...Productivity that produces what? Successful in what way, and for whom?...The moments [could be] ends in themselves, not steps on a ladder.”  (xix)

Distinction between remaining in place while escaping the attention economy and escaping the world. “I look to the history of refusal for responses that protest the terms of the question itself” The margin of refusal is shrinking. ( xxi)

“Simple awareness is the seed of responsibility.” “Patterns of attention are how we render reality for ourselves, and thus have a direct bearing on what we feel is possible at any given time.” 

### The Case For Nothing

“The practice of doing nothing, the architecture of nothing, the importance of public space, and an ethics of care and maintenance” (1) 

“Repressive forces don’t stop people expressing themselves but rather force them to express themselves.” “Nothing is neither a luxury or a waste of time, but rather a necessary part of meaningful thought and speech.” (4) 

“And perhaps that might be the way to write this book - to open the page and let the stories crawl in by themselves.” Symbiosis with art/creativity/story. Steinbeck, Cannery Row. (5)

We can actively structure our experience to be contemplative and full of wonder. “holds open a contemplative space against the pressures of habit, familiarity, and distraction.” (6)

Libraries, small museums, gardens, columbaria, “unfold secret and multifarous perspectives even within a fairly small area” (7)

Attention as a liquid flowing into environment. Labyrinths, stopping to listen, can collect our attention through interruptions. (Solnit, walking Grace Cathedral Labyrinth) (9)

Dad took two years off or work. “A lot of his anger about his job and outside circumstances had more to do with him than he realized.” Judgement and cynicism is about self. (11) “He said that leaving the confined context of a job made him understand himself not in relation to that world, but just to the the world, and forever after that, things that happened at work only seemed like one small part of something much larger.” Solitude can provide context.

Public spaces are perfect because “you don’t have to buy anything, or pretend to want to buy something, to be there.” (13) What they produce can’t be measured or exploited or even easily identified.

Personalization of economic risk. “Workers’ time exists, their time is there, permanently available to connect.” (15) Time “becomes an economic resource that we can no longer justify spending on nothing.” 

“The essentially cannibalistc nature of the gig economy dressed up as an aesthetic.” Work metastasizing throughout the rest of life.

“There is nothing to be admired about being constently connected, constantly potentially productive” Admiral critique of productivity culture. Informational overload as censorship (18)

“I consider doing nothing both as a kind of deprogramming device and as sustenance for those feeling too dissassembled to act meaningfully” (22)

Have to sit in tension between talking and listening. How can we have a take after having read a single headline. Berardi (Deep Listening) identifies tension between connectivity and sensitivity. (22) 

Abstraction of the body to just the mind loses information. “That which cannot be verbalized” is figured as excess or incompatible”

### Anatomy of a Farewell

“Appearing as if you’re doing nothing is seen as a threat to the general working order of the company, creating a sense of the unknown. The potential of nothing is everything.” (64) 

Diogenes was “Suspicions of distractions and education that prepared young people for careers in a diseased world rather than show them how to live a good life.” (66) 

“To pay attention to one thing is to resist paying attention to other things; it means constantly denying and thwarting provocations outside the sphere of one’s attention...It’s not just that living in a state of constant distraction is unpleasant, or that a life without willful thought and action is an impoverished one...A social body that can’t concentrate or communicate with itself is like a person who can’t think and act.” (81) 

“Meaningful acts of refusal have come not directly from fear, anger, and hysteria, but rather from the clarity and attention that makes organizing possible...Some can more easily afford to refuse than others.” (82) 

“Institutional support can go a long way toward alloign individuals to afford to refuse.” (83) 

Stanford students say that “Taking care of our own health is a guilty pleasure...We subliminally equate feeling burned out to being a good student.” (87) Duck syndrome. “The workaholism is driven by a very real fear of very real consequences that exist both within and outside of school. Students duly and expertly carry out complicated maneuvers in which one misstep - whether that’s getting a B or getting arressted for attending a protest - might have untenable lifelong consequences.” (88-89)

Idea of just “quitting facebook” does not shake individualism. It can be seen as a class taste. Still, “attention may be the last resource we have left to withdraw.” (93) We need the ability not just to withdraw attention but to invest it something else.

“If you can afford to pay a different kind of attention, you should.” “Creating landscapes in new dimensions that we can eventually inhabit with others.” (94) Persistent metaphor of attention as a space.
