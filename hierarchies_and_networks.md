# Hierarchies and Networks
## Paradigms of Temporality, Progress, and Ruination

Alternate Title: The Maze and the Trail

#toread: The Square and the Tower (Niall Ferguson)

Throughout the twentieth century, stories of progress carried Western civilization forward. Especially following the Second World War, all ordinary people had to do was march to the rhythm of expanding business, and they too would move forward. “Advancement, ever expanding, would move through them in tandem" (132, [[20200326221512]] the_mushroom_at_the_end_of_the_world). Even as global capitalism failed to bring the “end of history” or usher in universal prosperity, stories of decline or countermovements were often framed as the looking-glass twin of progress. In this way,  progress narratives continue to consume all: there are no leftovers, no excess, nothing remaining outside capitalism.

Activists, although often placing themselves in direct opposition to capitalist or colonialist systems, focused on individualistic and linear organizing: “Many align with the capitalistic belief that constant growth and critical mass is the only way to create change, even if they don’t use that language” (8, [[20200330113927]] emergent_strategy_brown). Nonprofits replicated the hierarchical structures of corporations. Scholarship “conspired against our ability to notice the divergent, layered, and conjoined projects that make up worlds” (22, [[20200326221512]] the_mushroom_at_the_end_of_the_world)

It is only in the past five years, with the increasing visibility of global climate change, long-term impacts to workers of the 2008 crisis, and political volatility worldwide, that these progress tales have seriously begun to lose traction. As Lowenhaupt-Tsing argues: “Progress is a forward march, drawing other kinds of time into its rhythms...Instead, agnostic about where we are going, we might look for what has been ignored because it never fit the timeline of progress” (21, [[20200326221512]] the_mushroom_at_the_end_of_the_world) In such disturbed human ecologies, there is space for new stories to arise, ones beyond Western civilization’s first principles (viii, [[20200326221512]] the_mushroom_at_the_end_of_the_world)

“It sometimes seemsthat that story [of heroism] is approaching its end. Lest there be no more telling of stories at all, some of us out here in the wild oats, amid the wild oats, amid the alien corn, think we’d better start telling another one, which maybe people can go on with when the old one's finished. Maybe. The trouble is, we've all let ourselves become part of the killer story, and so we may get finished along with it.” #quote #solace [[20200330205047]] hierarchies_and_networks

Dina Gilio-Whitaker argues that these progress narratives have much deeper roots; they are linked to the Western concept of linear time: “Time, as the primary organizing intellectual principle to which a spatial orientation is secondary, creates a linear and unidimensional world in which human existence is perceived as motion through space...an orientation that favors time over space in which the world is percieved in terms of progess based on forward motion naturally results in systems of hierarchy.” ([[20200330204247]] as_long_as_grass_grows_giliowhitaker, p.139) What if, instead, we saw ourselves walking a labyrinth instead of a trail? Instead of the path changing, what if the walker changes? [[20200331105258]] learning_to_walk_in_the_dark_browntaylor 

Furthermore, these systems of hierarchy inextricably bind colonialism and capitalism. Rosa Luxemburg’s third man theory argues that capitalist growth requires the continued existence of pre-capitalist sectors - or, as Tsing would put it, patches - in the economy. Once these patches no longer exist in the national territory, capitalists spread outward to other areas of the world. Hierarchies are unsustainable without feeding on spaces outside themselves; however, automatic collapse, so prophesied by the Marxists, is not possible until “the whole surface of the earth [is] conquered and all [has] been devoured” (39, [[20200326220830]] men_in_dark_times_arendt)

Even hopes of automatic collapse, however, are predicated on a linear notion of history and future events. Stories of ruination and revolution are predicated on previous progress. While such a global collapse is possible, a patchwork of postcapitalist and pericapitalist places, coming up in the disturbed soil unwanted by capitalist machinations, seems more likely. Livability, both in the social and ecological senses, is experienced only through places, uneven and unequal patches ([[20200326221713]] earth_stalked_by_man). Instead of seeking to escape capitalism entirely, another way exists: to look at the ecologies persisting through the cracks in capitalism: the “third nature” described in William Cronon’s work (viii, [[20200326221512]] the_mushroom_at_the_end_of_the_world #toread William Cronon's *Nature's Metropolis*). 

Our hierarchies of knowledge and narratives of progress have made it possible for a paradigm of domination to guide society. Emerging from these stories of violent competition and inevitable growth are patches of complexity, are connections and threads of adaptation and evolution. Activist and author adrienne maree brown said of Occupy Wall Street: “We are realizing that we must become the systems we need - no government, political party, or corporation is going to care for us, so we have to remember how to care for each other” (113, [[20200330113927]] emergent_strategy_brown). Like mushrooms, like dandelions, we must learn to notice the cracks and grow in them. After all, these patches may be the only hope we have against spreading unlivability.
