## Arts of Noticing

"I am not proposing a return to the Stone Age. My intent is not reactionary, nor even conservative, but simply subversive. It seems that the utopian imagination is trapped, like capitalism and industrialism and the human population, in a one-way future consisting only of growth. All I'm trying to do is figure out how to put a pig on the tracks." (17)

Promise and ruin as two sides of the same coin: "This is a story we need to know. Industrial transformation turned out to be a bubble of promise followed by lost livelihoods and damaged landscapes. And yet: such documents are not enough. If we end the story with decay, we abandon all hope - or turn our attention to other sites of promise and ruin." (18)

"We hear about precarity in the news every day. People lose their jobs, or get angry because they never had them...But most of the time we imagine such precarity to be an exception to how the world works. It's what 'drops out' from the system. What if, as I'm suggesting, precarity *is* the condition of our time - or, to put it another way, what if our time is ripe for sensing precarity? What if precarity, indeterminacy, and what we imagin as trivial are the center of the systematicity we seek?" (20)

Maze vs. trail: "Progress is a forward march, drawing other kinds of time into its rhythms...Instead, agnostic about where we are going, we might look for what has been ignored because it never fit the timeline of progress...[The story of decline shares] the assumption that the trope of progress is sufficient to know the world, both in success and failure. The story of decline offers no leftovers, no excess, nothing that escapes progress. Progress still controls us even in tales of ruination." (21) 

Definition: world-making projects which emerge from practical activities of making lives

Power of story, and the loss of it makes way for new tales of resilience: "Twentieth century scholarship...conspired against our ability to notice the divergent, layered, and conjoined projects that make up worlds...As progress tales lose traction, however, it becomes possible to look differently." (22)

## Some Problems With Scale

"Arts of noticing are considered archaic because they are unable to 'scale up' in this way." (37)

Scalable, controlled environments have disconnected components and alienation as a feature (plantations). As connectedness increases, scalability decreases but resilience can also increase. (39) Alienation is inherent to industrialization. But sometimes I want alienation? Sometimes it can be pleasurable?

"But it would be a huge mistake to assume that scalability is bad and nonscalability is good. Nonscalable projects can be as terrible in their effects as scalable ones. Unregulated loggers destroy forests more rapidly than scientific foresters. The main distinguishing feature between scalable and nonscalable projects is not ethical conduct but rather the latter are more diverse because they are not geared up for expansion. Nonscalable projects can be terrible or benign: they run the range." (42)

## Working the Edge

Definition: salvage is taking advantage of value produced without capitalist control. Salvage accumulation is the process through which lead firms amass capital without controlling the conditions under which commodities are produced. "Salvage is not an ornament on ordinary capitalist processes; it is a feature of how capitalism works. Sites for salvage are simultaneously inside and outside capitalism: I call them 'pericapitalist'." (63)

A key problem with anticapitalist critiques: unity and homogeneity are assumed. "Most critics of capitalism insist on the unity and homogeneity of the capitalist system; many...argue that there is no longer a space outside of capitalism's empire." (65)

## From Gifts to Commodities - and Back

"In capitalist logics of commodification, thigns are torn from their life-worlds to become objects of exchange. This is the process I am calling 'alienation', and I use the termas a potential attribute of nonhumans as well as humans." (121)

"Just as in factories workers are alienated from the things tehy make, allowing these things to be sold without reference to their makers, so too, things are alienated from the people who make and exchange them. Things become stand-alone objects, to be used and excahnged; they bear no relation to the personal networks in which they are made and deployed." Gifts vs. commodity as tool to explore what capitalism draws from noncapitalist value systems.

## Coming up among Pines…

“The way to manage a resource is to stop its autonomous historical action. As long as trees make history, they threaten industrial governance. Cleaning the forest is part of the work of stopping this history. But since when do trees make history?”

History is both a human storytelling practice and the set of remainders from the past that we turn into stores. “Modern forestry manages pines as a potentially constant and unchanging resource, the source of sustainable yields of timber. It’s goal is to remove pines from their indeterminate encounters, and thus their ability to make history.” (p.168) End of encounter-based time.

Note: p.170 has beautiful science writing on pines. I strive not to see prophecy in the world. Sun weak and anemic today.

On deep time and history: “Humans and pines have about the same length of history in Finland: as soon as the glaciers retreated, some nine thousand years ago, both humans and pines started coming. From a human point of view, that was a long time ago, hardly worth remembering. Thinking in terms of forests, however, the time line from the end of the Ice Age is still short. In this clash of perspectives, we see the contradictions of forest management: Finnish foresters have come to relate to forests as stable, cyclical, and renewable, yet the forests are open-ended and historically dynamic.” (p.172)

“Matsutake fruits best..with forty to eighty year old pines. There is no reason that Finnish Lapland’s managed pines, planned for hundred year harvest, that would not be thick with matsutake. The fact that in many years they are not is itself a gift: an opening to the temporal irregularity of the histories forests make. Intermittent, spasmodic fruiting reminds us of the precarity of coordination – and the curious conjunctures of collaborative survival.” (p.176)

“In the dilemmas generated by modern forestry’s stop-history efforts, conservationists have come to believe that forests need refugia from management. But these refugia will have to be managed if they are to survive. Perhaps one skill for the Zen arts of managed nonmanagement will be to watch pine’s partners rather than pine.” (p. 176)

## Resurgence

Resurgence vs. Resilience: Think past resilience to resurgence. “In the contemporary world, we know how to block resurgence. But this hardly seems a good enough reason to stop noticing its possibilities.” (p.179)

“We have stopped believing that the life of the forest is strong enough to make itself felt around humans.” (p.180)

“Future sustainability is best modeled with the help of nostalgia.” (p.182)

“Small disturbances eddy within the currents of big disturbances.” (p. 187)

“People and trees are caught in irreversible histories of disturbance. But some kinds of disturbance have been followed by regrowth of a sort that nurtures many lives. Peasant oak-pine forests have been eddies of stability and cohabitation. Yet they are often put into motion by great cataclysms, such as the deforestation that accompanies national industrialization. Small eddies of interlocking lives within great rivers of disturbance: these are surely sites for thinking about human talents for remediation…Despite all insults, resurgence has not yet ceased.” (p.190)

“Precarity is a globally coordinated phenomenon, and yet it does not follow unified global force fields. To know the world that progress has left to us, we must track shifting patches of ruination.” (p.206)

“If all forests are buffeted by such winds of destruction, whether capitalists find them desirable or throw them aside, we have the challenge of living in that ruin, ugly and impossible as it is.” (p.213)

“Brown’s advocacy for political listening inspires me to think past a disturbance in our aspirations. Without progress, what is struggle? The disenfranchised held a common program to the extent that we could all share in progress. It was the determinacy of political categories such as class – their relentless forward motion – that brought us the confidence that struggle would move us somewhere better. Now what? [Beverly Brown’s] political listening addresses this. It suggests that any gathering contains many inchoate political futures and that political work consists of helping some of those come into being. Indeterminacy is not the end of history but rather the node in which many beginnings lie in wait. To listen politically is to detect the traces of not-yet-articulated common agendas.” (p.254) 

Definition: **Latent commons**: “They are latent in two senses: first, while ubiquitous, we rarely notice them, and second, they are undeveloped. They bubble with unrealized possibilities; they are elusive. They are what we hear in political listening and arts of noticing.” (p.255) Potentially unreplicatable, they don’t institutionalize well. “Some radical thinkers hope that progress will lead us to a redemptive and utopian commons. In contrast, the latent commons is here and now, amidst the trouble. And humans are never fully in control.” “Privatization cannot wipe out the latent commons because it depends on it.” (p.267)

“Humans cannot control matsutake. Waiting to see if mushrooms might emerge is thus an existential problem. The mushrooms remind us of our dependence on more-than-human natural processes: we can’t fix anything, even what we have broken, by ourselves.” (p.257)

In Europe, administrators demand assessment exercises that reduce the work of scholars to a number…In the United States, scholars are asked to become entrepreneurs, producing ourselves as brands and seeking stardom from the very first days of our studies, when we know nothing.” (p.285)
