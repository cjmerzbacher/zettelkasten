# Patchy Apocalypses: Quilting the Anthropocene

On my good days lately, I’ve been thinking about starting a quilt. Maybe it’s because I’m home, wrapped in one my grandmother made. We’ve always had a few of those floating around our house, usually gifts for special occasions - my birth, my parents’ marriage - but since her passing we’ve slowly unfolded the others we inherited and packed into a box to ship from her house in North Carolina. The one I’m under now isn’t my favorite - it’s a rather cookie-cutter strip box pattern in cornflower blue and goldenrod, machine quilted - but it still reminds me of her, and of the courage and persistence I hope to one day have. 

Patrick Maloney has a spoken word poem called “Lighthouses”. It begins:

> 		i have two grandmothers.
> one gets out of bed every morning as if life were a lover beckoning her forward
> at 80 years old she still leaves the house more than I do
> she will die before me, but I will not outlive her
> the other grandmother
> she doesn’t get out of bed anymore
> she sat still too long letting the waves lap at her ankles
> she turned into driftwood
> she was swept out with the tide
> abandoned her body in a nursing home where it no longer recognizes its own children
> i am heir to both of these women

My Oma had terminal cancer for seven years. During that time, she ran a half marathon (after double knee replacement surgery), became her retirement home’s bocce champion, baked dozens of pies and thousands of cookies, and beat all her family members at board games a hundred times over. During the last few months of her life, however, she mostly did two things in her spare time: reading paperback mysteries and sewing quilts. 

On my bad days lately, I wish for a second Flood, some kind of total apocalypse that wipes the slate clean. It’s a fantasy, I know, one that I’ve entertained for years on and off in moments of depression. It curls like smoke around my dreams on nights when I fall asleep thinking of war, or climate change. In January, I dreamt I woke and looked out the dormer window of my San Francisco apartment to see the city gone, obliterated by nuclear weapons dropped by Iran. In October and November, I dream of fires.

Now we’re in the apocalypse, the inevitable turning over of the world long prophesied but never actually enacted. And I am realizing two things: that my catastrophes are fantasies, and that the patchy reality is much harder to live with. 

Perhaps I should be separating my terms here. As Laurie Penny writes: “A catastrophe is total devastation, with nothing left and nothing learned. “Apocalypse”—especially in the biblical sense—means a time of crisis and change, of hidden truths revealed. A time, quite literally, of revelation.” The dark parts of my brain hoped for a catastrophe; instead, we have an apocalypse on our hands. 

John of the Cross speaks of *la noche oscura*, the dark night of the soul that frees oneself from one’s ideas, fears, and attachments about God. My catastrophes were depression; when they pass, all is restored. When these dark nights pass, all will be transformed. [[20200331105258]] learning_to_walk_in_the_dark_browntaylor

Livability has been thinning throughout the Anthropocene. But now it seems to be threadbare everywhere, like a sock worn thin until the entire toe gives out. I’m not sure why I want to make a quilt. But my mother showed me the one she’s started, stitching together bits of fabric she finds around the house with mismatched, colorful thread. Maybe I want to feel myself growing something new beneath my fingers out of the scraps left of my life. Maybe I just want to cover my head and retreat inside myself.
