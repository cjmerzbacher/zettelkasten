- Jorge Luis Borges, *On Exactitude in Science*: A map as large and detailed as the empire itself. A parable for how too much knowledge can become impractical.
- Deeper problem: "adding detail only gives you more of the same kind of information - more roads, more mountains, more villages - when what you might need is a completely different schematic."
- Scientific paradigms can be thought of as a map, often unifying with a simpler set of principles with knock-on implications.
	- Example: Maxwell's equations
- AI is not currently built to do this: it predicts within existing frameworks. This risks *hypernormal science*.
**CM: AI is only as good as the data we have now. It can only imagine the world as it is now. What would a deliberately fabulative AI look like? How would that give us access to futures we don't currently have, expanding our adjacent possible?**
- New paradigms often benefit from a willingness to step outside the establishment.
- Foundation models try to sidestep labels by training on raw data; however, they can only fill in unexplored spaces on existing maps (like amino acid sequenecs) rather than ask if those paradigms are correct in the first place.
- Optimizing performance on existing benchmarks makes it difficult for alternatives to emerge
	- Example: Passing simulated peer review would likely not work for truly new ideas; which often face significant pushback.
- AI gravitates towards problems rich in existing data, rather than searching for new ground to cover.
**The Bitter Lesson, Richard Sutton**
- Alternative approaches
	- Symbolic regression (AI Feynman)
	- Analogies 
	- Grounding AI reasoning in physical experience (multimodal architectures, self-driving laboratories)
- "Some analogies are not between written theories, but rather between an idea and a sensory intuition."
- Institutional protection to pursue ideas that look unproductive by conventional measures (self-play?)
