# Annotations

> \[This book] builds upon the concept of assemblage first sketched by [Gilles Deleuze and Félix Guattari](deleuze-and-guattari-assemblages.md)

> All assemblages, he explains, are singularities, they are historical and individually unique, and are therefore not "particular members of a general category"

LCU: DeLanda wants to map the "individuation process that has given rise to an assemblage." So I guess we understand "individuation" to be a sort of ungeneralizable generation.

CM: Latour's actor-network theory - entity defined by interactions with other entities
    LCU: Karen Barad is also a good draw for this idea

> DeLanda's assemblages consist of parts that retain autonomous and indepedent of their network \[...] therefore assemblages are characterized by "relations of exteriority."

CM: relations of
    - interiority (defines identity)
    - exteriority (nonconstitutive of component parts)

> Assemblages are characterized by the "emergent properties" that are "produced by the interactions between components." As such, the nature of assemblages are not defined by "essences"

LCU: Directly contravening the [British emergentists](emergence-encyclopedia-philosophy.md), thank god

> assemblages and their components possess mind-indepdendent agency \[...] we must regard assemblages as "legitimate agents"

CM: assemblages as agents or components of assemblages as agents?
LCU: Both, especially given that the components of assemblages *are* assemblages themselves.
LCU: Ball does also recognize that [Benett](bennett-vibrant-matter.md)'s exploration of non-human agency is more rigorous.

> DeLanda's assemblage theory is a flat, materialist ontology of immanence. It is flat because "the ontological status of all assemblages is the same," and immanent because all assemblages "populate the same ontological plane"

LCU: I think "immanence" here corresponds to our use of "emergence," but they really aren't interchangeable. For a phenomenon to arise within a system is different than for a phenomenon to arise at a "higher order" because you pretty much have to throw out reductionism. I wonder if that's why DeLanda emphasizes each assemblage's uniqueness - we don't want to separate out the [external conditions](weak-emergence.md) because the assemblage's relations with its historical contingencies are the point.

> reality consists of assemblages all the way down.

> All of existence is composed of a "nested set" of assemblages that operate at varying levels of scale, terminating in "the grand cosmic assemblage, teh plane of immanence"
