# General notes

The Poietic Generator is a game in which multiple people generate an image together. Each person contributes 20x20 pixels, and the image evolves as each of them changes their section of the image in response to emerging patterns at the macro-scale. It was designed by Olivier Auber in 1986, and a whole lot of people have had things to say about it ever since.

> the Poietic Genrator is perfectly transparent: "everything is known or knowable"

LCU: Major distinction from Conway's Game of Life and most agent based models (as described by [Bedau](weak-emergence.md)). The authors here are referring to what the agents know - since the agents are the human players, though, that means they're also the observers. I wonder if this is relevant to what you've been thinking, Andy. 

AP: Yes, this connects directly to [Gu et al.](gu-more-really-is-different.md) The Poietic Generator is almost the experiential version of their proof. Gu et al. show that even with complete knowledge of the microscopic Hamiltonian, you can't generally predict macroscopic observables. The Generator shows the same thing from the other direction: every player has total visibility of the system, and emergence still happens. Full information doesn't give you predictive power over collective behavior. The agents are the observers, as you say, and they still can't anticipate what forms will appear. That's weak emergence in action, not because the rules are hidden, but because the computation is irreducible.

> The Poietic Genrator positions itself as a metagame which invites a questioning of the processes of social interaction, especially when they are mediated by technological devices interacting with social networks. \[... the Generator] could contribute to "a certain conceptual knowledge (dianoia) of how the doxa is formed and exercised on us, especially through technology".

LCU: The quote here is from Oliver Auber, the designer. The "doxa" is a person's imagination and judgment, which can be shaped by a system's framework. That's pretty clearly connected to [Wynter](wynter-unsettling-decoloniality.md)'s epistemic regimes, but it's also very particular to social systems that give the appearance of being flat. There's a real difference between a state's power and a platform's power, even if they both make themselves invisible. Still, I appreciate the framing that the Generator challenges the power of the social rules by making them visible. (I will say that I'm not sure we've seen any effects of that challenge in the 40 years since the game came out, even if the point has become more and more relevant)

AP: Most systems that shape doxa (social media algorithms, recommendation engines, ad targeting) do so precisely because their rules are invisible. The Generator inverts that by making the entire infrastructure legible to every participant. That's actually close to what Wynter is calling for: not just resisting an epistemic regime, but making the regime visible so one can interrogate it. Fair point that it might not have produced an epistemic shift, but I think as an early exercise it at least raised new interesting questions.
