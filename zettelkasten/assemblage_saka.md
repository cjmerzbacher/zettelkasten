> "The so-called theory of the 'middle range', in which they create concpets for their purposes by deriving them from the alternative authority of macro counter-discourses that invest in the emergent and the heterogeneous"

CM: Another interesting word: collage.

> "Assemblage generates enduring puzzles about 'process' and 'relationship' rather than leading to systematic understandings of these trops of classic social theory and the common discourse that it has shaped. It offers an odd, irregular, time-limited object for contemplation. Whoever employs it does so with a certain tension, balancing, and tenativeness where the contradictions between the ephemeral and the structural, and the structural and the unstably heterogeneous create almost a nervous condition for analytic reason."

CM: An assemblage of definitions

> "Assemblage is an experimental genre form that is thus organic to the contours of the object of study."

CM: Literature on assemblage often takes a messy, heterogeneous form mimicing its objects, but this is not always successful.

> "Assemblage is the source of emergent properties of what Deleuze and Guatarri call machinic processes."

> "Their properties are emergent in the sense in which that concept is deployed in logic that is not part of and so not forseeable in light of either one or the other system considered in isolation, but instead only discernable as a result of  the intersection of both such systems."

Playfully and critically aesthetic vs. formal and technical (math, set theory, topology)

> "The one indulges and even celebrates the intractably unpredictable and contingent in rapidly changing contemporary life; the other hopes for an understanding of the structural principles of order (and disorder) within the play of events and processes."

Global assemblages (Aiwha Ong and Stephen Collier, 2004) about study of globalization using principles of anthropological research.

> "If pushed too far, if insisted upon too literally - if it becomes anything more than an allusion - assemblage rapidly becomes a dead metaphor in one's work."
